# Petal Archive

*A design philosophy for ceremonial documentation*

---

## I. The Act of Signing

Consent is intimate. The moment a client traces their signature onto the surface of an agreement is not bureaucratic — it is the threshold between imagination and permanence. The interface must understand this. It must recede with grace, offering space rather than spectacle, warmth rather than gloss. The screen becomes paper. The form becomes ritual. Every field, every checkbox, every gentle progress dot is a quiet breath before the mark is made.

Form speaks through restraint. Space is the primary material. Surfaces are warm, not cold — cream and blush pulled from memory of old correspondence, of letters sealed with wax. Glass dissolves into paper. The only structural element that announces itself is the rose — a single color, used sparingly, like ink on parchment. Everything else yields to content, to the human filling the form.

---

## II. Surface & Material

Paper is the foundation. Not the simulation of paper, but its emotional truth: warmth, grain, weight. Backgrounds pull from a palette of cream (`#FAF5F0`), warm ivory (`#FDF8F6`), and deep blush (`#FFE4ED`) — never cold, never clinical. A radial gradient anchors the composition from the upper quarter, as if light enters through a tall studio window and pools across the surface. There are no floating orbs, no animated gradients. The background is still.

Cards are white, luminous, and quiet — lifted from the surface by a shadow so soft it is felt more than seen: `0 4px 28px rgba(0,0,0,0.055)`. Borders are hairline, drawn in rose at 8% opacity: `rgba(206,86,124,0.08)`. No saturation filter, no blur stack. The glass era passes. What replaces it is the meticulous craft of a surface refined through patient reduction — every element placed as if by hand, every margin considered for breath. The result appears as though it took countless hours to arrive at this simplicity.

---

## III. Typographic Ceremony

Type is the soul of the document. `Noto Serif Display` at large scale commands the emotional register of each screen — italic for the secondary half of every heading, roman for the first, as if two voices speak in unison. Kerning is generous. Line height is slow, unhurried. The size shifts dramatically across hierarchy: H1 at `clamp(2rem, 8vw, 3rem)`, labels at `0.75rem`, legal text at `0.78rem` — the distance between these scales creates the sense of depth, of a designed artifact rather than a generated form.

Step indicators are typographic. Roman numerals — I, II, III, IV, V — appear as large, near-transparent letterforms behind the step header, printed in rose at 6% opacity. They are felt before they are read. Progress is shown as a thin horizontal track, a precise line in rose that grows from left to right with painstaking exactness. Each transition is 400ms, `ease-in-out`. Nothing jumps. Nothing bounces. The craft is in the stillness.

---

## IV. Color as Ritual Mark

Rose is the only color that speaks. `#CE567C` — deep, warm, neither aggressive nor sweet — appears at three moments only: the active state of any selection, the call-to-action button, and the italic accent in every heading. Everything else is neutral: soft charcoal text `#2A1A1E`, quiet secondary text `#8A7A78`, borders in faded blush. The lime and yellow accents of the main site do not enter this space. This is a different room — quieter, more serious, still beautiful.

The primary button carries the full weight of rose, rendered as a gradient from `#CE567C` to `#e0517a`, with a shadow that glows softly below: `0 6px 24px rgba(206,86,124,0.28)`. It is meticulous in its execution — every padding, every radius, every letter-spacing calibrated through sustained attention. The button does not shout. It simply stands, confident, at the bottom of every step like a closing signature.

---

## V. Botanical Presence

Nature enters at the margins, never at the center. A single botanical motif — fine-line organic curves, drawn as an SVG data URI at 4% opacity — appears behind the hero text of the opening screen. It does not decorate; it texttures. Like the grain of paper under ink, it exists to be felt, not seen. On all subsequent screens, the botanical disappears entirely. The form is clean. Only on the completion screen does a trace of it return, behind the checkmark, as if the ritual has left its quiet impression on the surface.

This restraint is painstaking. The temptation to decorate is resisted at every turn. What remains is the product of deep expertise — the knowledge of what to remove. The composition earns its authority through what it withholds.

---

*Petal Archive is not minimalism. It is ceremony distilled.*
*It is the visual language of a studio that understands that beauty is not added — it is revealed.*
